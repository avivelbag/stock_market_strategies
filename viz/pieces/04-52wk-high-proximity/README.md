# 52-Week High Proximity — visualised

An interactive backtest of the anchoring-bias strategy in
`strategies/04-52wk-high-proximity/`.

## What it teaches

George & Hwang (2004) is one of the most-replicated anomalies in behavioural
finance: stocks *near* their 52-week high keep drifting up because investors
anchor on that high and under-react to good news beneath it. The rule goes long
when `close / 52-week-high ≥ 0.95` and rising, exiting below `0.90`.

The honest lesson here is about **what a backtest can and cannot show**. The
George & Hwang effect is *cross-sectional* — it ranks thousands of stocks
against each other. Applied to a single isolated synthetic series, the sorting
mechanism is gone, so the in-sample numbers are negative and the walk-forward
is uninformative (the 252-bar warm-up exceeds the fold length). The page states
this plainly rather than hiding it: a strong thesis can still fail to be
demonstrable on the wrong test.

## What you see

1. **Price, signal & trades** — close, held-long shading, ▲/▼ markers.
2. **close / 52-week-high panel** — the proximity ratio with the entry (0.95)
   and exit (0.90) threshold lines.
3. **Strategy equity vs buy & hold** — net-of-cost, both from ×1.00.
4. **Metrics + interpretation** — the computed sentence explicitly flags when
   the test, not the thesis, is the limiting factor.
