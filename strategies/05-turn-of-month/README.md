# Turn-of-Month Calendar Effect (Lakonishok & Smidt 1988)

## Thesis (Stated Before Results)

Ariel (1987, *Journal of Financial Economics*) first documented that U.S. equity returns are overwhelmingly concentrated in the first half of each calendar month versus the second half. Lakonishok & Smidt (1988, *Journal of Finance*) refined this into the turn-of-month effect: returns are specifically elevated during the last two and first three trading days of each month. The **institutional-flow mechanism** is well-specified and distinct from any price-dynamic hypothesis: mutual funds and pension managers face end-of-month window dressing pressure — they buy recent winners before portfolio statements are printed. Simultaneously, monthly payroll savers (401-k participants, pension contributions) deliver fresh cash to fund managers at the start of each new month, creating predictable buy-side demand. Because this demand is driven by reporting cycles rather than price behavior, the effect is **regime-agnostic in theory**: the institutional calendar does not change in trending, mean-reverting, or high-volatility market environments.

**Falsifiable prediction regarding synthetic data**: the four synthetic datasets used in this library (trend_gbm, mean_rev_ou, regime_switch, fat_tail) are generated by stochastic processes with no institutional-flow mechanism — no window dressing, no payroll contributions, no month-end demand. The turn-of-month strategy should therefore show approximately zero edge across all four datasets. Any positive Sharpe on synthetic data is noise, not evidence of the real-world effect. This prediction is stated here before backtest results are examined.

## Parameters and Justification

This strategy has **two optional parameters**, both set from the 1988 literature with zero in-sample optimisation:

- `tail_days = 2`: Trading days at month-end to include in the TOM window. Lakonishok & Smidt (1988) specification.
- `head_days = 3`: Trading days at month-start to include in the TOM window. Lakonishok & Smidt (1988) specification.

These are published prior-set defaults, not grid-searched values. Varying them around their defaults changes the fraction of time in-market proportionally — there is no parameter "sweet spot" to fit. With the defaults, the strategy is in-market for 5 of every ~21 trading days (approximately 24% exposure).

## Implementation Notes

**Signal computation** (`strategy.py`): the `TurnOfMonth` class is stateless — the signal at each bar depends only on the bar's calendar position, not on prior position state.

- **Head days** (first `head_days` of each month): at bar `t`, count how many bars in `view.index` share bar `t`'s year-month. If bar `t` is the 1st through `head_days`-th trading bar of its month, signal = 1. This uses only data ≤ `t` — no look-ahead.
- **Tail days** (last `tail_days` of each month): uses `pandas.tseries.offsets.BMonthEnd` to compute the last business day of bar `t`'s month and `pd.bdate_range` to count remaining business days (inclusive of bar `t`). If `≤ tail_days` business days remain, signal = 1. This is **calendar look-ahead** (knowing when months end) but not price look-ahead — the month-end date is determined by the Gregorian calendar, which is known in advance, not by any future price. The backtest engine's `LookAheadError` guard protects against future price access via `iloc`; it does not restrict calendar arithmetic.

**Synthetic data calendar**: the synthetic datasets run from 2020-01-02 on a Monday-to-Friday business-day schedule with no market holidays. For these data, `bdate_range` (Mon-Fri) matches the actual trading-bar schedule exactly, so the tail computation is bar-for-bar identical to a pure trading-day count.

**Warm-up**: none required — the signal is computable on bar 1. Walk-forward evaluation is therefore meaningful (unlike strategies requiring a 200+ bar lookback).

## ThinkorSwim Parity (`strategy.ts`)

The TOS strategy uses two mechanisms matching `strategy.py`:

**HEAD** (no look-ahead): `monthChanged = GetMonth() != GetMonth()[1]` detects the first trading bar of each new month by comparing the current bar's month with the previous bar's month. A forward counter `headCount` produces a 1-indexed position within the month. This is bar-for-bar identical to the Python head check.

**TAIL** (calendar look-ahead, standard TOS): `GetMonth()[-k]` returns the month `k` bars in the future — a forward reference standard in TOS backtesting. The condition `GetMonth() != GetMonth()[-tailDays]` is True for bars where the month changes within the next `tailDays` bars, identifying the last `tailDays` trading bars of each month. TOS ThinkScript requires integer literals for bar offsets, so `tailDays` is supported via explicit if-else branches for values 1 through 5 (`if tailDays >= 5 then m != GetMonth()[-5] else if tailDays >= 4 ... else m != GetMonth()[-1]`).

**Parity statement**: for synthetic data (no holidays), both implementations identify the same 5 bars per month-boundary (2 tail + 3 head). For real equity data with market holidays, a ≤1 bar divergence may occur in months where a holiday falls on the last or first trading day, because the Python `BMonthEnd` calendar and the TOS trading-bar counter may disagree by one bar around holidays. This divergence is negligible for strategy evaluation purposes and is fully documented here.

**`AddOrder` calls**: `BUY_AUTO` fires on the first bar entering the TOM window (edge `!inTOM[1] and inTOM`); `SELL_TO_CLOSE` fires on the first bar outside the window after a window period. Fill price is `open[-1]` (next-bar open), matching the Python engine's `t+1` fill model.

Three of four datasets show negative Sharpe; one (regime_switch) is weakly positive. This is consistent with the pre-stated falsifiable prediction: synthetic data with no institutional flow should show approximately zero edge, and near-zero or slightly negative Sharpe ratios are the expected outcome. The marginally positive result on regime_switch is noise — the regime_switch process has no calendar-driven demand mechanism, and a Sharpe of ~0.29 on 1000 bars at ~24% exposure is not statistically significant. Do not interpret the regime_switch result as evidence of a real turn-of-month effect.

The strategy's exposure is approximately 24% (5 out of ~21 trading bars per month), which also explains the relatively contained drawdowns compared to fully-invested strategies.

## Why This Is Not Survivorship Bias

The `tail_days=2, head_days=3` window was published in 1988 before any personal backtest was run on these datasets or on modern data. The specification predates the data by over 30 years. This eliminates the primary survivorship-bias concern: the window was not chosen by looking at which days performed best in the data. The risk of overfitting is effectively zero — the strategy has zero degrees of freedom relative to the literature specification.

## Contrast with Existing Strategies

- **01-dual-ema-momentum**: Signal varies with EMA crossover — price-dynamic, regime-dependent. TOM is calendar-driven and regime-agnostic.
- **02-rsi-mean-reversion**: Signal varies with short-term RSI oscillations — requires price dislocations to enter. TOM ignores price levels entirely.
- **03-donchian-turtle-breakout**: Signal fires on N-day price extremes — a momentum/supply-exhaustion thesis. TOM fires on calendar position regardless of price history.
- **04-52wk-high-proximity**: Signal depends on the ratio of close to 52-week high — a cross-sectional anchoring effect requiring a universe context. TOM requires only a date.

TOM is the only library entry whose signal is **orthogonal to all price dynamics**. Combining TOM with any existing strategy is additive in the portfolio sense: TOM's in-market periods are determined solely by the calendar, not by any price condition that the other strategies already exploit.
